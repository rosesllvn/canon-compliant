Chapter 61

The Gift

A Certain Someone (某某) by Musuli — fan translation

Old Man Ding had one of those phones for the elderly, the characters on it as big as bronze bells. The story went that Jiang Tian had once wanted to buy him a normal smartphone and had patiently promised to teach him until he could use it. But the old man absolutely refused, saying his eyes were too dim and dazzled to make out a single character on those smartphone screens.

The old man was a menace, threatening that if Jiang Tian bought one he'd just turn around and resell it—and he was entirely capable of doing exactly that. So Jiang Tian, unable to win, had no choice but to buy him the elderly-special model. The thing a kid would turn his nose up at, the old man loved; once it was in hand, it never left his side.

Jiang Tian got prickly about it, and the old man loved teasing him, often showing it off to people—my Xiao Tian bought this for me and so on. Naturally he'd shown it off to Sheng Wang too. Jiang Tian had been sitting right there eating at the time, his face going flatter the more he ate, until at last he stuffed a big drumstick into the old man's bowl and said, "Eat. Don't talk."

The old man gripped his chopsticks and made to swat him, saying he had no respect for his elders and a rotten temper. Sheng Wang nearly died laughing beside them.

The old man's phone could store family numbers—convenient, and just in case of an emergency. Jiang Tian had taken slot No. 1, and the old man said that was enough. Later Jiang Tian had a word with Xile and added Boss Zhao's in too. After Sheng Wang came, things shifted a little: he took No. 2, and Boss Zhao got bumped to No. 3.

Under normal circumstances, though, Old Man Ding still only ever called Jiang Tian, so Sheng Wang was a little surprised when the call came through.

The old man said, "Ji Huanyu's come bothering me again. Drag Xiao Tian off to eat somewhere else, don't let him come over."

That was a strange thing to say, and Sheng Wang listened to it with some puzzlement. "Grandpa, you mean don't tell him Ji Huanyu's here?"

"Obviously, or I'd just call him directly," the old man said, out of sorts.

Old Man Ding's lying on the phone was always especially obvious. Afraid of being questioned, he'd deliberately pitch his tone fierce and hang up in two or three sentences, giving the other person no chance to speak.

Never mind Jiang Tian—even Sheng Wang could tell, clear as day.

Sheng Wang gave an "Oh."

The old man went on, "I'm afraid that if he hears Ji Huanyu's name, his mood'll go sour again."

That much was true. Sheng Wang had witnessed Jiang Tian's face change. Back when Jiang Ou had brought it up too, his mood had visibly turned bad.

It was a little odd, actually, and Sheng Wang had never quite figured it out.

He couldn't help asking, "Grandpa, why does Jiang Tian find him so unbearable?"

At first Old Man Ding didn't catch his meaning, and said as a matter of course, "Ji Huanyu's no good, is he? What difference does having a father like him make over not having one? Of course it's perfectly normal to find him unbearable."

"No, I know that." Sheng Wang chose his words carefully. "But if it's about neglect—from the things you've told me, Grandpa, actually…"

The truth was, Jiang Ou and Ji Huanyu were two of a kind, both negligent in caring for the young Jiang Tian. The difference was that Jiang Ou's was a matter of having no choice, and Ji Huanyu's was simply his nature.

But Jiang Tian's attitude toward the two of them was worlds apart. Toward Jiang Ou, though he wasn't as close as an ordinary mother and son, he was at least protective—he'd be concerned, his heart would soften. Toward Ji Huanyu he was utterly repelled, unwilling even to spare him a glance or waste a word on him.

Hearing Old Man Ding talk about Jiang Tian's childhood before, Sheng Wang had wondered whether Ji Huanyu used to hit him. But later that didn't seem right either, because Jiang Tian wasn't the least bit afraid of Ji Huanyu.

When the two of them appeared together, it was Ji Huanyu who was more careful, if anything. And not a careful that was out in the open, but… he seemed afraid that some sentence might prod one of Jiang Tian's land mines. Jiang Tian, by contrast, had no fear of him, not a trace—only weariness.

Or, to put it more strongly, disgust.

Old Man Ding couldn't explain it clearly over the phone either; he hadn't been going into Jiang Tian's home in those years, after all, and didn't know exactly what kind of rift had grown between father and son. Like Sheng Wang, he was working off guesses.

But Jiang Tian was so hard to guess…

Sheng Wang thought.

"So what's he doing over at your place?" Sheng Wang asked.

Old Man Ding gave a snort. "What else. He knows Xiao Tian doesn't mind an old man like me, that the kid's close with me, so he's come to use me as a go-between. Probably figures I'm old and muddled at this age, easy to fool—he puts on a respectable act and I'll decide he's a good sort. Or maybe he figures if he butters me up a bit, Xiao Tian won't find him so unbearable."

Sheng Wang found it rather absurd—a biological father living to the point where he had to win his son back through dutiful kindness to an old neighbor. That was a kind of talent too, he supposed.

"A go-between for what?"

"For making up." Old Man Ding sighed. "A man over forty, drifting around aimlessly, and suddenly he remembers he's got a son and wants to clear the air with Xiao Tian and reconcile."

"Wasn't he abroad before?" Sheng Wang said.

"Yeah. I heard a classmate of his, or a friend, came down with some serious illness—don't know if it's cancer or what. He probably got a bit scared thinking about it. People are like this once they reach that age, prone to dwelling on things—when you're young this doesn't matter and that doesn't matter, and then you start to regret it. You see someone else fall ill and you think, what if that's me one day, and if there's not even one person close to you by your side, well, that's pretty miserable too."

But the young Jiang Tian hadn't had a single person close to him by his side either.

Sheng Wang retorted inwardly.

The old man smacked his lips, grumbling in displeasure. "Even raising a cat or a dog takes spending time together, building up a bit of affection. Him, though—all these years, and he doesn't know how unbearable Xiao Tian finds him? Counting on a couple of grinning, fawning attempts to smooth it all over—what kind of dream is he having? And he still wants to take the boy abroad. Hah—"

The old man gave a cold snort. "I'll be the first one to say no!"

Even after he'd hung up, that line—and he still wants to take the boy abroad—kept echoing in Sheng Wang's head. Though he knew Jiang Tian wouldn't give Ji Huanyu the time of day, it still bothered him a little.

That night they ate at the cafeteria.

Thanks be to Gao Tianyang—this melon-rind of a person walked into the cafeteria with such an unbridled gait that he accidentally stepped on the sudsy cleaning cloth one of the cafeteria aunties had laid down, sat down hard on his rear, and slid several meters along the floor.

Every classmate behind him laughed themselves sick.

Sheng Wang, who'd still been a little down, couldn't hold it back this time either. He bent over laughing for a good while before realizing he'd reflexively slung an arm across Jiang Tian's shoulder—and that Jiang Tian was laughing too.

Gao Tianyang sat on the floor rolling his eyes and stuck out a hand. "Laugh at your grandma. Can't one of you come help me up? I provided a brief moment of joy to your repressed lives, the least you can do—you've really got no sense at all!"

Song Sirui was laughing so hard he was tipping over. Sheng Wang went over to lend a hand, and the crowd hauled him up in a tangle of arms.

"Hey, are my pants wet?" Gao Tianyang craned around to look at his backside.

"Eh, it's okay. You didn't wet yourself much," Song Sirui said.

"I swear I'll yank your pants off and swap them, you believe me?" Gao Tianyang raged.

"Nope. They won't fit you."

"I—"

Gao Tianyang, thoroughly aggrieved, clapped a hand over his behind and sat down with everyone else. He said, "Sheng-ge, I know you're a good person. I want the sweet-and-sour pork ribs, curry beef brisket, and spicy diced chicken from Window 8. Can you get them for me? If I don't get to eat them I'm going to die in agony right here today."

"???"

Sheng Wang turned to look at the long dragon of a line that had wound around two corners and was now backed up to the cafeteria's front door, and asked, incredulous, "Why do I like you so much?"

Gao Tianyang blew him a flying kiss. "Because I'm this charming."

The words had barely left his mouth when his campus card was plucked out of his hand.

Jiang Tian pinched the card between two fingers and gave it a little shake at him, asking calmly, "I'll buy. Say again what you want."

Gao Tianyang: "……"

He said, "I want the baby greens, steamed egg custard, and pork chop from Window 3."

Jiang Tian said, "Wait here."

Everyone laughed themselves sick again.

Apart from the wildly popular Window 8, the other windows actually had plenty of people too. Sheng Wang and Jiang Tian queued up at the tail of Window 3's line, and Song Sirui and the others tagged along, giggling.

The line wasn't crowded, but the presence of the person behind him was still very strong. Sheng Wang pinched a corner of his campus card and absently fanned himself with it, when he suddenly heard Jiang Tian ask, "Are you very hot?"

"……"

What a conversationalist.

Sheng Wang's movements paused, and he stuffed the card into his pocket—at which point a certain someone's presence grew even stronger.

"Are the teacher's lessons okay?" Jiang Tian's low voice came again, very calm, not sharp and cutting like it had been at Wutongwai.

"They're pretty good," Sheng Wang answered.

Having said it, he felt the answer was a little dry, and added, "A bit easy, but still pretty good."

It was a good while before he heard Jiang Tian respond: "Mm."

A meal really did go by fast. Gao Tianyang and the others belonged to the wolf-it-down faction, and however genteel Sheng Wang was, there was no dragging it out too long.

They went back to Mingli Building and parted ways at the third-floor landing. As Sheng Wang stepped into the Class B classroom, he felt his heart slowly sink again, like a hot-air balloon whose burner had gone out.

It was only then that he realized how happy he'd just been.

The happiness lasted only a little while, and then he had to spend the entire evening study session, even longer, getting himself to cool down.

Five minutes traded for five hours, one hour traded for a whole day, and every day after that was this same process, repeating over and over.

Without his noticing, the time he spent eating grew shorter and shorter, and the time he returned to the dorm after evening study grew later and later.

In the whole grade, only Class A had the privilege of staying in its own classroom for self-study. Students from every other class had to gather in the lecture hall.

At first, when Sheng Wang shouldered his bag and left, there were still more than half the people in the room packing up. The next day it became fewer than half, and after that only a scattered few, and finally only himself.

By the time he got back to the dorm, lights-out was often not far off. Before he could get a couple of sentences out, the whole dorm would fall silent under the lights-out signal.

He'd lie with his eyes closed listening for movement from the bunk below, turn over a few times, and drift off to sleep without realizing.

Though he kept telling himself he didn't want a cold war or distance with Jiang Tian, that it was only a brief struggle with himself—

it was nonetheless an almost foreordained process. As much as he didn't want to admit it, he and Jiang Tian were still, unavoidably, walking off in two different directions.

This week's weekly exam at the affiliated high school was temporarily canceled because the city was running a master-teacher showcase. Several Senior Grade 2 classes were pulled to record lessons on Saturday and Sunday, while the other classes had ordinary self-study.

Sheng Wang pulled out his usual heap of problem sets and worked through them from the moment he opened his eyes until night fell. As he carried a fresh English-competition workbook into the lecture hall, Shi Yu finally couldn't help himself: "Damn, that's the third one, isn't it?"

"Third what?" Sheng Wang sat down in the rearmost corner, pulling out books as he spoke.

"This week I've watched you finish two competition problem sets this thick, this is the third—aren't you tired?" Shi Yu got a headache just looking at them.

But Sheng Wang blanked for a moment. "Have I?"

"You don't know how many problems you've done yourself?"

"Didn't really pay attention."

Far from not paying much attention—he wasn't even picky about the quality of the problem sets. As long as there was something to fill his idle time, that was enough. The busier the better.

The corner of Shi Yu's mouth twitched, and he gave Sheng Wang a thumbs-up. Because lately Sheng Wang was downright frightening; sitting next to him chatting on WeChat had even started to make Shi Yu feel a little embarrassed, and these past few days he'd inexplicably started doing problems too.

Frightening to say, he was already half a workbook in—genuinely industrious as never before.

"If the weekly exam hadn't been canceled, I feel like I could've shot up a few ranks," he said, half-smug, half-modest. Pity he got no reply.

Sheng Wang had already plugged in his earbuds and started on his problems.

Shi Yu watched a while and felt the other was in a strange state. As if extraordinarily focused, and yet somehow absent-minded.

……

The bell ending evening study rang right on time. Shi Yu and Qiu Wenbing had both packed their bags; they'd grown used to Sheng Wang's late returns, so they said goodbye and headed back to the dorm first.

The enormous classroom slowly grew empty and spacious again.

In his earbuds the track happened to switch to a very old English song, the singer's husky voice low and gentle. Sheng Wang blanked for a second, then remembered he'd lifted this one from Jiang Tian's playlist.

Maybe it was just unlucky timing, but every previous time the song had come up it had been daytime, with noise and clamor all around, making it seem too dull and quiet. Only in this moment did he discover it was actually really good.

Sheng Wang sat a while, wrote down a few words with his head bowed, and finally stopped his pen again. From outside came the sudden sound of voices: two boys dribbling a basketball went by, scuffling and snatching at it, the pounding of it echoing down the corridor.

Some teacher passing by gave an angry shout, and the two of them dutifully hugged the ball and ran off—you could still hear them laughing from a long way off.

Sheng Wang drew his gaze back, then suddenly pulled out his earbuds and hurriedly packed up his pencil case and books. He didn't know what had come over him; it was just that in this instant, he very much wanted to go back.

So he slung his backpack over his shoulder and ran with long strides toward the dorm building.

Sheng Wang reached the sixth floor at 10:45, a fair bit earlier than the past few days. As he pushed open the dorm door, he met his roommates' startled eyes.

Qiu Wenbing asked, puzzled, "What's wrong, Sheng-ge? What are you running in such a hurry for?"

Shi Yu said, "This early today?"

But Sheng Wang answered neither of them. His gaze swept across the lower bunk, the desk, even the washbasin and the bathroom—and didn't catch sight of the other person anywhere.

He braced a hand against the door, caught his breath, set his bag on the desk, and asked, with feigned offhandedness, "Where's Jiang Tian?"

"Not back yet," Qiu Wenbing said. "Doesn't he always come back around eleven?"

Sheng Wang blanked.

Qiu Wenbing then caught himself. "Oh, right—you used to come back a minute or two later than him. Makes sense you wouldn't know."

In that moment, Sheng Wang found it hard to describe how he felt. He was dazed for a few seconds, and felt his heart pricked by something, very lightly and very heavily.

Without his knowing from which day, he no longer knew Jiang Tian's daily routine.

"He…" From the running, his voice had gone a little dry and hoarse. He paused before saying, "Why's he so late too—is he being studious?"

"Don't know, prepping for a competition maybe?" Qiu Wenbing said honestly. "I've seen him copying out something lately, looks like notes and problems."

Sheng Wang nodded.

He stood by the desk a while, then felt the wind go out of it. After circling twice, he tugged at his collar and said, "I'm going out to the balcony for some air. Ran back and now I'm roasting."

"Oh," Qiu Wenbing said. "Keep an eye on the time, Sheng-ge, lights-out's coming."

"I know."

The balcony had a sink, useful for washing large items like clothes and bedding, and the dorms also used it to rinse mops and draw water.

Sheng Wang slid the balcony door shut, fanned himself, then leaned and sat down on the rim of the sink, propping himself on the white porcelain ledge and letting his head drop.

He'd run too hard and was worn out; he wanted to rest a bit, he needed to catch his breath.

A very, very long time passed before he heard the muffled sound of talking from inside the dorm, and a moment after that the balcony door clicked open and someone walked in.

Sheng Wang kept his head down. He knew who it was, but for the moment he couldn't muster the energy to smile. He felt a little wretched.

For no reason at all.

Jiang Tian didn't ask him what was wrong, didn't ask why he was sitting there.

The balcony was very quiet. He merely stood in front of Sheng Wang, probably looking down at him the way he always had.

After a long while, Sheng Wang pressed his lips together, fixed his expression, and lifted his head to attempt a joke. "I'm out here getting some air, what are you coming over to block it for?"

But the words done, he saw Jiang Tian holding a thick leather-bound notebook.

"I've been blocking it a while now," Jiang Tian said, setting the thick notebook down by his hand and tapping a fingertip on the cover. "For you."

"What is it?" Sheng Wang blanked. He picked up the notebook and turned a couple of pages, then found he couldn't quite keep turning.

He'd seen something like this before—back when he'd twisted his ankle and was rotting from boredom at home, Jiang Tian had gone through different books and compiled a heap of interesting problems for him.

That batch had been just like this: book titles marked, page and problem numbers marked, written out clearly as to what made each problem special and why it was worth picking out to study.

But this time was a little different. The contents of the one before him now were even more meticulous. He didn't have to go hunting—the problems had been cut out, one by one, and pasted flat and neat into the notebook, sorted by category, with notes alongside on their special points and merits.

The latter half of the book had the corresponding answer keys and explanations, matched line by line.

Jiang Tian said, "You said the teacher doesn't dig deep enough. With these added in, it should be enough."

He'd picked them all out problem by problem, all three of the math-physics-chemistry subjects among them. However far he himself could study, Sheng Wang could go just as far—whether or not it counted as a crude little gift.

He wouldn't take anything from anyone else. He only gave. He'd only ever sift and pick through what he had himself, drawing out whatever could be drawn out for the person he cared about.

Sheng Wang said he'd failed, so Jiang Tian would pull him back up. Sheng Wang said the teacher's lessons were too easy, so Jiang Tian would fill in the rest.

This was the most practical thing he could think of.

So…

Jiang Tian looked at him and asked, "Can you test your way back?"
