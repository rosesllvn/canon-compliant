A Certain Someone

Ch. 81 · A Certain Someone

"Neighbor"

某某 by Mu Su Li · fan translation by rosesllvn

Only now did Sheng Wang discover he was a giant in words and a dwarf in deeds. Day to day he teased Jiang Tian with perfect ease, yet now, over one sentence, three little words, he was thrown into utter rout.

In the end the two didn't dare stay in the living room and went back up to the second floor.

It was winter, plainly, yet the room was sweltering. The AC hummed away, and Sheng Wang felt his brain had drawn close to its frequency.

He lay back, the eyes beneath the back of his hand a little damp. His lashes flickered open and shut, his focus slipping in wave after wave of blankness.

Jiang Tian's breathing was heavy too. He pulled a few tissues and was about to wipe his hands when Sheng Wang pinned him.

A boy this tall was actually quite heavy. Sheng Wang said hoarsely, "I almost thought I was the only one who couldn't take being teased. Courtesy demands reciprocity—I'll help you too."

It was the first time Sheng Wang had seen Jiang Tian like this: eyes half-shut, Adam's apple flushed red, his gaze falling from under half-lowered lids, sharp and disordered.

This is mine alone—no one else gets to see it, he thought.

The room seemed hotter still, the back of his own neck and ears burning too. He narrowed his eyes, tightened his fingers, and said to Jiang Tian, "Ge, I want to photograph you like this."

Jiang Tian bent one leg and closed his eyes. After a moment he opened them again, reached out to cup the back of Sheng Wang's head, and turned to kiss him.

❖

The next morning Sheng Wang was startled awake by a commotion on the stairs.

Jiang Tian had already thrown back the quilt and sat on the bed's edge, listening to the sound outside with a frown. He fished out his phone for a glance at the time and asked, lowering his voice, "What day do they get back?"

Sheng Wang was still mired in the haze of just opening his eyes, and blanked several seconds before grasping that Jiang Tian meant Sheng Mingyang and Jiang Ou. "Thursday."

His throat was badly hoarse; having spoken, he picked up the cup at the bedside and gulped some water—then froze mid-motion, nearly spilling a bedful.

The talking downstairs wasn't very clear, but he could still make it out: it really was Sheng Mingyang and Jiang Ou.

"Why are they back today?!" Sheng Wang rolled upright, raked at his hair, and hurried down.

He didn't even put on slippers, padding barefoot across the carpet to the door, meaning to peek out quietly—only to open it and find someone in the bathroom across the way.

Auntie Sun, mop in hand, paused at the sight of him. "Did Auntie wake you?"

Sheng Wang was a little dazed. "Auntie, why are you in this bathroom?"

"The downstairs tap's broken." Auntie Sun finished and said, surprised, "Eh? Xiao-Tian? You slept on this side last night too?"

Only then did Sheng Wang remember there was a person behind him, and he nearly slammed the door shut on reflex. Luckily Jiang Tian was much calmer. He picked up his jacket, patted Sheng Wang's shoulder, and turned sideways past him out of the bedroom, telling Auntie Sun, "We talked about things too late last night."

"Hm?" Sheng Wang blanked, then echoed, "Mm."

Anyone who knew Jiang Tian extremely well would know that explaining at such length was actually a little out of character. Luckily Auntie Sun didn't see him every day and wasn't familiar enough with him for that, so she heard nothing off. As for Sheng Wang, he was always half a beat slow just after waking, and Auntie Sun was used to it.

"I just saw the guitar in the living room," Auntie Sun said.

Sheng Wang blanked again, thinking: not good—in last night's muddle they'd gone up and never put the guitar and the rest away. On instinct he explained, "I dug it out, told him before I'd teach him guitar. Then we got to talking about all the classes I took as a kid, and… and I brought him up to see the certificates, forgot to clear up the stuff downstairs."

Auntie Sun laughed. "How old are you, already reminiscing about childhood?"

Sheng Wang gave a dry laugh. "Ah… yeah, reminiscing about childhood."

Jiang Tian's steps toward the room next door paused; he shot a glance at him, then turned the handle and went into his own bedroom.

Sheng Wang shrank back too, and with a head of sleep-tousled hair circled the room twice, aimless.

Leaving a guitar downstairs was no big deal, and two brothers sleeping in one room wasn't that strange either. The main thing was that Auntie Sun had washed the mop and was bustling about cleaning—she asked those questions and forgot them, paying them no mind at all.

Once he'd changed clothes and brushed his teeth, the cool minty rush cleared his head considerably and restored his usual state, and he felt none of it was any big problem after all.

The fluster tossed to the back of his mind, last night's events surfaced instead. So one step out of the bathroom, Sheng Wang turned back and splashed two more handfuls of cold water on his face.

Droplets at his brows and the corners of his eyes, too lazy to wipe, he simply leaned on the sink scrolling his phone while waiting for his face to dry.

The screen lit up nonstop, new messages dropping in one after another. He skimmed them roughly and tapped into Moments—and happened on a fresh post.

It had been posted one minute ago, and in that little time the comments had already lined up into a long dragon, the content much of a muchness: either "Damn, the sun rose in the west, look what I scrolled into," or "Are my eyes failing, Tian-ge actually posted to Moments," with Gao Tianyang, Song Sirui and a few other clowns picking up the relay of "Today is a fine day."

Jiang Tian, whose Moments were utterly bare, had broken precedent and posted first thing in the morning. The content was very simple: a shared guitar rendition of a song called "Childhood."

Class monitor little Carp asked below: You planning to practice this one? That's great, this is exactly one I don't go off-key on.

A few other classmates chimed in below that it was good, simple and nice to listen to.

Only Sheng Wang knew a certain someone was obliquely poking fun at the line he'd given Auntie Sun—"reminiscing about childhood last night."

Because of that share, Sheng Wang splashed his face twice more, then dropped a comment at the bottom of that long dragon.

Say That Again: Go teach yourself.

A few seconds later, Gao Tianyang replied to him: Fierce little brother.

Song Sirui jumped in right after, only the moment he'd finished copying, Gao Tianyang deleted his and changed it to: Fierce Sheng-ge.

Big Song: …you playing me?

The two clowns made Sheng Wang laugh, so he was fairly relaxed by the time he went downstairs.

He'd actually been a little afraid of facing Sheng Mingyang and Jiang Ou, so he'd dragged his feet not wanting to go down. But reaching the living room he found Jiang Tian had sat down on the sofa a step ahead of him, and his heart suddenly settled.

"Didn't you say you'd be back Thursday? Why home today all of a sudden?" Sheng Wang asked.

Sheng Mingyang said, "It was supposed to be Thursday, but with something that big happening at Fuzhong's gate, I had to come back and see for myself to feel easy. And I heard the old gentleman who takes you to lunch is sick?"

"You even know that?" Sheng Wang exchanged a glance with Jiang Tian, surprised. "I don't think I even mentioned it to you."

Sheng Mingyang laughed. "I know quite a few people at Fuzhong—is it so strange to be well informed?"

That had been part of the reason for transferring Sheng Wang here in the first place. Sheng Mingyang knew a fair number of people at Fuzhong, which made it convenient to keep an eye out. It was Sheng Wang himself who'd forgotten the fact.

He stilled a moment, then gave an "oh."

Sheng Mingyang didn't notice that instant's oddness. "So how's the old gentleman now?"

"Taken to the hospital, a trace of cerebral hemorrhage, admitted for an IV. He's awake, doctor says it's not serious," Sheng Wang answered.

Having relied on Old Man Ding's care so long, with the old man now sick, the two parents couldn't not visit. So that afternoon, the party of four made a trip to the hospital.

This hospital was famous for neurology, with crowds surging through every day; only the inpatient wing was quieter.

Several buildings of varying heights were cradled by a man-made lake and gardens, connected by covered walkways—a fine place to convalesce. By the lake and in the gardens, family members pushed wheelchairs, taking patients out to clear their minds, in twos and threes.

Sheng Mingyang carried a great heap of gift boxes of food and supplies, and with Jiang Ou's introduction got chummy with Old Man Ding in a few words, soon talking and laughing freely.

Jiang Ou took the ward's empty kettle out to fetch hot water, saying she'd wash a couple of persimmons to peel along the way. As the people in the room talked, the topic turned again to the woman fished out by Fuzhong's gate.

The matter had nothing to do with them, really, but old folks just loved to fret, picking up a line here and a line there to pass the time. In a city this size, cases like this were neither many nor few. Before any conclusion came, it would always become the talk of the whole district, and so rumors flew, every sort of thing said.

The old man in the next bed said mysteriously, "I went down for a stroll just now and heard people say that woman's been claimed."

"That's good," Old Man Ding nodded. "It'd be a pity if no one ever claimed her. Though this family's something else too—a grown person gone and they didn't even know?"

"No," the old man waved a hand. "Not claimed by family. By another woman."

"Another woman? What do you mean?"

"A friend?" Sheng Mingyang wasn't keen on chatting about this sort of thing, but he'd play along and slip the old folks the odd line.

"Hardly!" The old man waved again, then crooked his two thumbs. "This kind of relationship."

Sheng Mingyang hadn't caught on; the old man clicked his tongue and spelled it out: "Not ordinary friends at all—a couple!"

"Two girls?" Sheng Wang blanked.

"That's right!" The old man shook his head. "They say the one who's gone wasn't on the straight and narrow, running around outside, family cut ties with her. This time she'd taken on loan-shark debt or made some enemy, anyway—" He smacked his lips and shook his head. "No good, and messing around with a woman besides—what's it called, homo—"

"Homosexual?" Sheng Mingyang supplied.

Sheng Wang had been getting a little bored listening to them and had thought to pull Jiang Tian out for a turn. Then the word leapt out of his dad's mouth, and he stiffened on the spot.

He shot a quick glance at Jiang Tian, then turned to look at Sheng Mingyang—who still wore his gentle, refined air, listening to the old man pronounce his verdict: "Right, pretty perverted."

The hand at Sheng Wang's side went cold.

Face pale, he scrubbed hard at his fingertips, and on instinct wanted to argue the old man down—but the moment he opened his mouth, Jiang Tian tugged him.

Sheng Wang frowned, thinking Jiang Tian meant to pull him out and pretend they hadn't heard. But the other only drew him a step back and spoke himself: "Isn't it a bit much, talking about someone like that?"

He'd always been blunt, and Old Man Ding and Sheng Mingyang both knew it, so the line out of his mouth seemed natural enough. The old man blanked at the question, and Sheng Mingyang quickly smoothed it over: "True, the person's gone, and who knows how it really was—we're not the police, are we?"

Old Man Ding, on the other hand, had stayed silent the whole time, utterly quiet. Only when he followed Jiang Tian downstairs did Sheng Wang realize the old man had never once taken part in the talk about "homosexuality."

He suddenly had an intuition: that Old Man Ding, though he'd never said so, might have long known about certain things with Ji Huanyu—only the old man's attitude was a little strange…

To be precise, Old Man Ding's attitude toward Ji Huanyu had always been a little strange. Not like a simple neighbor's—no neighbor would point at Ji Huanyu and curse him the way the old man did, nor, having cursed, dig out an old album alone to look at old photos.

Just out of the elevator, Sheng Wang suddenly grabbed Jiang Tian. "You're the one who registered the old man at the hospital, right—you've got his social security card?"

Jiang Tian asked, puzzled, "Why ask that?"

"Can I take a look at it?"

"It's not on me."

"Oh." Sheng Wang thought, then asked, "Do you know the old man's actual surname?"

Jiang Tian hadn't expected the question. He was silent a moment. "Ji."

Sheng Wang's steps braked to a halt.
