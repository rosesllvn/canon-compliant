Chapter 59

Class Shift

A Certain Someone (某某) by Musuli — fan translation

The whole period of physics, Class A sat under a kind of low-pressure front. Of course it wasn't entirely because of Sheng Wang alone—but he was undeniably the main cause of it.

He Jin usually worked a few not-very-funny jokes into her lessons, but today she was serious from start to finish. She broke down the reasoning at the podium while the students scribbled away below. Sheng Wang didn't take down much, because his phone screen kept lighting up—messages, one after another.

Gao Tianyang and Song Sirui, the two motormouths, were sending the most, and Sheng Wang kept switching back and forth between the two chat windows until he finally couldn't keep up and just pulled them both into a group.

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: No!!! I damn well still can't accept this!!!

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: Whyyy……

Big Song: I'm having a hard time accepting it too

Big Song: This isn't right

Big Song: Lao Gao got in

He was clearly fishing for a fight. On any normal day Gao Tianyang would've sparred with him for half an hour, and the mood would've livened right back up. But today Gao Tianyang just took the hit.

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: Yeah, even I got in

Sheng Wang typed away with his head down, dragging out the same thing he'd already explained: I told you before, I didn't do well.

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: That's just you being modest!!!

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: When the test's over you ask ten people, all ten will say they didn't do well, it's just a thing people say isn't it???

Sticker: I never say things just to say them

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: ……

Big Song: ……

Big Song: Huh, that's actually true

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: Big damn true

Sheng Wang really never said empty face-saving things. When he said "so-so," it meant he wasn't too happy with how he'd done; when he said "okay," it meant he'd done pretty well; when he said "really good," it meant it was genuinely great.

And that was already the toned-down, modest version. With Jiang Tian he got even cockier.

Once, holed up in the next bedroom over organizing notes, he'd even shot his mouth off: "Just wait—within one semester, I'll be able to poke the tiger's backside."

Jiang Tian had blanked for a second back then, and asked what he meant.

Sheng Wang said, "First place is the tiger king of the mountain. Second place is close enough to reach over and give it a poke."

The tiger had probably never met anyone so brazen in its life; it took a solid two seconds to digest the joke. First it gave Sheng Wang a long, hard-to-describe look, then it hauled him out of the bedroom by the scruff, books and all, and said, "Dream on. That'd be faster."

Gao Tianyang and Song Sirui were still going. Sheng Wang's fingers hovered over the keyboard while he zoned out. That whole exchange had only been a month or two ago, and yet thinking back on it now, it felt oddly hazy.

His "study" had been empty for a long time; the place they lived had already changed. And jokes like that, the reckless kind—he wouldn't be making those anymore either.

Because he had a guilty conscience.

It was just a class shift, not some life-or-death farewell—only moving from the floor above to the floor below. Gao Tianyang and Song Sirui had the timing of a comedy duo, and after Sheng Wang threw in a couple of interruptions and they cracked a few more jokes, the mood quickly turned lively again.

Big Song: Next shift is at finals, Sheng-ge's a lock to fight his way back in

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: Absolutely!

Sticker: Lao Gao I suggest you make the most of your time

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: Why should I make the most of my time?

Sticker: If you're still sitting at rank 45, next time when I come back in, you're the one who'll be crying

Plain-and-Simple Gao Tianyang: ?????

The dummy must have just caught on, because he fired off a whole row of dumbstruck stickers, then quietly put his phone away and went back to taking notes. And so the consolation session ended in a reverse-kill and a lecture on studying harder.

Sheng Wang backed out of the little group and found twenty-some unanswered messages waiting, from all sorts of people in the class. Some told him it was fine, plenty of people cycled in and out of Class A. Some said that at the rate he was improving, next time he came back he'd be a permanent fixture. And some, who weren't very good at comforting people, just sent a few emoji.

And that was only part of them.

He'd replied to every WeChat message and looked up to find several little folded notes had appeared on his desk—more of the same consoling words, the content much alike, the handwriting all different. Sheng Wang didn't even know who'd tossed which one over, but that didn't stop him from being a little moved.

The kind of dumb, simple friendships that only existed at fifteen, sixteen.

He also saw Little Pepper crumple up a pale-pink note and, the moment He Jin turned around, fling it backward without looking—only for it to land on Gao Tianyang's desk. And that dummy Gao Tianyang, not catching on at all, started signing back and forth with her, the two of them somehow holding a whole note-passing conversation like a chicken talking to a duck.

Sheng Wang found it hilarious.

He ducked his head and laughed under his breath a couple of times, then slowly let the laughter fade. It suddenly occurred to him: would Jiang Tian look at him the way he was looking at Little Pepper—keeping a knowing distance, never letting it get awkward, never giving anyone the wrong idea?

But that was obviously impossible.

Ordinary people didn't drift onto the same wavelength as him, so there was never any chance of that knowing understanding. And Jiang Tian was family—he couldn't exactly keep an ordinary classmate's distance, either.

He only wanted to steer a path that had gone crooked back straight again. He had no intention of cutting Jiang Tian off.

Sheng Wang gave a self-mocking little smile and thought, this is honestly worrying me to death.

The more worrying thing was that he'd gotten messages from nearly everyone in Class A—everyone except one person, who had stayed silent this whole time.

He looked at the chat pinned to the top of his WeChat and felt the smallest flicker of panic.

The physics period went by unexpectedly fast, as if in the space of two blinks, and then the bell rang. Sheng Wang was startled back to himself by the sudden sound. As He Jin stepped down from the podium, he glanced back at the seat behind him and met Jiang Tian's eyes head-on.

For some reason, Sheng Wang's first instinct was to bolt. And then he actually did.

Aren't you a coward.

Sheng Wang spat at himself inwardly. He chased after He Jin's retreating figure into the office, delivering himself early to the scolding. Sure enough, his "Reporting in" was like a lamb walking into the tiger's den; five teachers swarmed him in an instant.

"Perfect timing, I was just looking for you!"

The Chinese teacher, Zhaocai, whipped out a test paper and shook it in front of Sheng Wang. "What is this reading comprehension you've done here? How many times have I said it—for reading comp and poetry analysis you look at the points, look at the points, look at the points! An eight-point question, nine times out of ten the answer has four key points. A six-point question, three. Any fewer and it's definitely wrong. To be safe you can pad it out to eight little points or six little points—it's not like extra costs you anything. You know this routine cold, so how'd you capsize this time?"

"And the recitation. How many times have I told you lot—when you're memorizing, don't just move your mouth, pick up a pen and write it out. One wrong character ruins the whole thing. Doesn't matter how smoothly you can recite it, it's all wasted."

Zhaocai had barely finished when Yang Jing slapped down a paper of her own in front of him, jabbing at the multiple-choice questions she'd marked up. "Were you out of your mind, or did you go numb from underdressing those couple of cold days? You made errors this basic?!"

No matter how much she liked a student, Sister Jing didn't pull her punches when she got going. If anything, the more she liked you, the fiercer she got.

Seeing Sheng Wang dutifully bow his head and take it, Zhaocai softened and put in a word for him: "Leave the English alone, the kid still came first, didn't he."

"Oh, first place is impressive, is it?" Yang Jing said. "Is it me who's never seen a first place, or him?"

Zhaocai: "……"

"Don't you play good with me!" Yang Jing rapped her knuckles on the desk—knock, knock. "You tell me yourself whether you'd have gotten these wrong if you'd just looked one more time!"

Sheng Wang made an "Mm."

"Mm my foot!" Yang Jing said. "Just thinking about it gives me a stomachache."

Old Wu and the others were flipping through papers off to the side, their expressions far gentler—not like Yang Jing, who looked ready to jab Sheng Wang in the forehead while she scolded him. But their mood was much the same—

You couldn't say Sheng Wang had answered carelessly; he hadn't, really. Most of the questions were answered quite well. Only a small portion fell below his level, and even the score wasn't ugly—it was within the normal range of fluctuation.

Pull any single subject out on its own and Sheng Wang's marks weren't bad at all; every mistake could be called a small slip. But add up the slips across all five subjects, and it became a real shame.

They thought it over and over, and all they could land on was: such a shame.

"If you hadn't missed these, your English total alone would've been at least five points higher! And five points—do you know what five points means?" Yang Jing said. "Five points and you wouldn't have to change classrooms, you know that?"

"I'm sorry," Sheng Wang said.

Of course he knew that if he hadn't gotten these wrong he wouldn't have to switch classrooms. He'd gotten them wrong precisely because he knew. He didn't regret it—as long as it was something he'd chosen himself, he rarely regretted even the wildest of it. But he did feel guilty. Very, very guilty.

"All right, all right. Lucky it's only one midterm, there'll be chances later." He Jin had seen many cohorts of students through, and not one of them lacked its share of the brilliant and outstanding—but every one of them had its own way of giving her grief.

Adolescence was a compound of impulse and accident to begin with: the most compelling thing in the world, and the most infuriating. As a homeroom teacher, she was used to it.

Compared to the subject teachers, He Jin paid attention to more than just the marks. She was more like another kind of parent.

She pulled out a chair and said to Sheng Wang, "We've done the scolding. Sit."

"You twisted your ankle a while back, and missed a few of the small quizzes." He Jin had a neatly clipped stack of forms in hand, each student's progress, regression, and points to watch noted in red pen. Sheng Wang's column had a great deal written in it.

"This time your grade rank is 49, and 147 across the four schools. Compared to the test before you hurt your ankle, that's actually progress. But whether that progress took a week or a month—that makes a difference." He Jin's voice was gentle. "I'm not pushing because I think you aren't good enough. It's because you're good enough that I want you to perform at the level you should. And that level shouldn't be 49, or 147."

"I get the feeling your state hasn't been great lately. Is something on your mind?" He Jin looked into his eyes.

Sheng Wang lowered his gaze, then after a moment calmly looked back at her, smiled, and said, "Nothing's on my mind. It won't happen again, teacher."

"Fine." He Jin finally let her expression ease and made a joke. "Director Xu over at the Political and Educational Office told me earlier—you, you just trade on your looks. You look so well-behaved, the model student, but really you're a handful. I'll take you at your word this once, all right? Next exam, let me see you climb inside 45. Can you do that?"

"Yes," Sheng Wang nodded.

"And if I catch you slacking off even once—" He Jin pointed a finger at him and gave a little snort. "—then you can wait for a face-to-face."

Yang Jing gestured in a circle and added, "See that? Five teachers. Wheel-formation, no-mercy, face-to-face."

Sheng Wang laughed.

By the time all of that was done, the ten-minute break had been used up exactly. Sheng Wang walked back to Class A alongside He Jin, and the bell rang right on time as he entered the room.

He hurried back to his seat, and to his surprise, the seat behind him was empty.

Sheng Wang held out for a bit, then couldn't help patting Gao Tianyang on the shoulder.

"Huh?" Gao Tianyang turned around, puzzled.

Sheng Wang jerked a thumb behind him. "Where'd he go?"

"You mean Tian-ge? Convenience store," Gao Tianyang said.

The words had barely left his mouth when Jiang Tian twisted open the front door of the classroom and said, without lifting his eyes, "Reporting in."

He Jin tipped her chin toward his seat, signaling him to sit down quickly—then her gaze passed over his hand twice in a row, and at last she called out to him, puzzled, "Jiang Tian."

Jiang Tian happened to be passing right by Sheng Wang's desk. His steps paused, and he turned his head toward the podium.

He Jin asked, "You're drinking iced water in this weather? Aren't you cold?"

"Not cold." When Jiang Tian turned back, his gaze swept across Sheng Wang's face. He carried the fogged-over bottle to the seat behind and sat down. His shirt brushed lightly past Sheng Wang's shoulder, trailing a thread of icy air.

Sheng Wang didn't look back. He heard the sound of a cap being twisted open behind him, and though it was Jiang Tian drinking, somehow it felt as if he'd swallowed a few mouthfuls too.

Iced water in late autumn had to be shockingly cold.

For the entire rest of the morning, Jiang Tian didn't say a word. Only when the last class ended did he stand by Sheng Wang's desk, umbrella in hand, and tap a finger against the desktop. "Let's eat."

Road No. 3 was as long as ever. The two of them walked side by side under one umbrella, their pace unhurried, and neither of them spoke. Passing a trash can, Jiang Tian dropped the emptied bottle in.

That bottle was still beaded with water right up until it was tossed; the knuckles of his fingertips were a bloodless white, cold just to look at. Sheng Wang suddenly wanted to test the temperature, but couldn't find any reason to.

A scene like this reminded him of the first time they'd gone to Xile—Jiang Tian had been wordless the whole way then, too. Back then he'd taken it for granted. Now it just felt deeply unfamiliar.

"Ge," Sheng Wang called to him.

If Sheng Mingyang had heard that word, he'd probably have been moved beyond measure. After all, back then, no matter how he'd coaxed and cajoled, Sheng Wang had refused to so much as let it past his lips.

The truth was, he still wasn't used to saying it. But he was trying.

He was lazy by nature; it was rare for him to try this hard—even if this particular effort brought him no joy.

The bone at the side of Jiang Tian's face shifted slightly. His expression gave nothing away. After a moment he finally looked over.

"Are you angry with me?" Sheng Wang asked.

Jiang Tian's gaze rested on his face a while before he drew it back. "No."

Sheng Wang nodded, and only after another long pause answered, "Oh."

They rounded the corner of the long alley and stepped one after the other over the threshold of the old courtyard. Old Man Ding came out to meet them, brandishing a spatula. "Quick today, eh—no dawdling on the walk."

"Yeah." Sheng Wang played along. "I'm starving."

Starving to the point that his stomach was cramping with it.

"Good timing, I made a chopped-chili fish head today." The old man was full of self-satisfaction. "Heard the cafeteria's done it before? You two taste and tell me which is better."

The old man was in a fine mood—not only chopped-chili fish head, but also a stewed silkie soup and three stir-fries. The red and green of the chopped chili and the jade-green flowering greens were laid out neat and tidy; the mute uncle was there too, happily fetching bowls and chopsticks.

"Weren't you starving? Eat more." Old Man Ding heaped their bowls full of rice and ladled out the soup, then waited, delighted, for the verdict.

Sheng Wang praised it up and down, praising the old man into full bloom.

He turned and asked Jiang Tian, "Well? Tastier than the school cafeteria, isn't it?"

Jiang Tian gave an "Mm."

"Oh, so you think it's tasty too?" Old Man Ding eyed him sidelong. "And here I thought I'd poisoned it."

Jiang Tian finally raised his head to look at him, a question on his face.

Old Man Ding pointed at his own cheek. "It's tasty—so what are you doing looking like the world's wronged you?"

Jiang Tian dropped his eyes and swallowed his food, and only after two seconds said, "Eat smiling, and you'd be even more likely to ask me what's wrong."

Old Man Ding actually found that fair enough. He pictured the scene and gave a shiver. "Forget it, forget it. Eat."

Sheng Wang's stomach hurt, and the truth was he couldn't really taste anything. But since he'd said he was starving, he still ate more than usual. The old man and the mute ate fast, half a bowl down in a couple of rough mouthfuls, and soon finished first and went off to the kitchen to deal with the vegetables left over from the morning.

That left only the two of them in the hall.

Sheng Wang ate slower and slower, and finally set down his chopsticks.

Jiang Tian's soup spoon clinked against the rim of his bowl with a soft sound. He suddenly spoke. "Stomach hurt?"

It took Sheng Wang a few seconds to register that Jiang Tian was talking to him on his own initiative, and his mood lifted at once. Reflexively he said, "No, I'm just full."

Jiang Tian said nothing. He drank two more mouthfuls of the chicken soup, head down, and finally couldn't hold it in: "Is that how you lied to Lao He in the office too?"

Sheng Wang went stiff. This time he was genuinely frozen in place.

Maybe afraid his tone had come out too cold, or too aggressive, Jiang Tian kept his eyes down the whole time, only waiting in silence for an answer, the white porcelain spoon pinched between his fingers without taking another sip of soup. But even so, those sharp, edged corners of him still showed through.

Just like that bottle of late-autumn iced water—the body of it wrapped in a layer of soft, hazy mist, and yet cold enough to sting the hand.

Sheng Wang shifted, meaning to change position, but the ache in his stomach made him too lazy to bother.

"Lied to Lao He about what?" he asked.

Jiang Tian: "About failing on purpose."

Sheng Wang's stomach clenched, a needle-prick pain spreading swiftly outward. He bowed slightly at the waist and for a long while couldn't get a word out.

This stomachache really had impeccable timing, he thought, mocking himself; it probably looked exactly like he was faking.

He pressed down hard on the painful spot twice and said to Jiang Tian, "I didn't do it on purpose. Why would I fail a major exam on purpose—there's nothing in it for me."

The whole class was consoling him, thinking he'd had an off day, that his luck had been terrible. All the teachers were lecturing him, thinking his state was poor, that he'd been careless. Only Jiang Tian knew he'd neither had an off day nor been careless—he'd done it on purpose.

He couldn't find a reason, couldn't find any proof. He just knew.

Jiang Tian pressed his lips into a straight line, knit his brows once, as if he wanted to say something, and as if he didn't know what to say.

"I didn't do it on purpose." Sheng Wang's gaze dipped slightly, his voice very low.

There was no color in his face, whether from not resting well or from the stomach pain. The old house had poor light, the hall was dim, and outside the rain came down hard, water sliding down the slanting roof and hanging a curtain of it off the eaves.

Jiang Tian found himself thinking, for no clear reason, of the first time Sheng Wang had gotten drunk—sitting sullen in the car, his face just like this, glancing now and then out the window, the light and dark of it streaming in patches across his half-lowered eyes, sometimes very bright, sometimes only the faintest pinprick of a star.

He never said much, and yet he always seemed a little lonely.

It was probably from around then that Jiang Tian had started being unable to help treating him a little better. And then, without noticing, it had become habit.

Jiang Tian stood up from the table. The person who'd just been making excuses suddenly caught him by the wrist.

"What is it?" Sheng Wang asked, looking up at him.

"……"

Jiang Tian moved his fingers. "Getting hot water."

Sheng Wang said "Oh," dropped his gaze again, and let go.

Jiang Tian went to the kitchen, dug out a glass and rinsed it, poured half a cup of boiling water, then mixed in a little of the cooled boiled water the old man had set out. He came back to the hall and set the cup in front of Sheng Wang.

"When are you moving?" he asked.

"Hm?" Sheng Wang hadn't caught up.

He repeated it, patient: "When are you switching classrooms?"

"Noon." Sheng Wang paused, then added, "Before the lunch break ends, probably."

There wasn't actually much time left, but neither of them brought up leaving. The hall sank into a long silence, and Sheng Wang picked up the cup and drank the slightly-too-hot water in small sips.

After a long while, he suddenly said, "I really did do badly. There were a lot of mistakes in every subject."

Tell that to the ghosts.

That was what Jiang Tian thought. What he said was, "Okay."

Sheng Wang drank a few more mouthfuls of hot water. Maybe the stomach pain had eased a little; his color had improved somewhat.

Jiang Tian was quiet for a moment, then nodded once more and said, low, "Okay."

The lunch break at Mingli Building was always quiet, but today it was noisy—you could hear the scraping of desks and chairs from down below. At first it sounded lively, but it was a case of some rejoicing while others grieved.

When Sheng Wang got back to the classroom, the other four students who needed to switch rooms had already packed their bags; one of them was empty-handed, having clearly already made a trip downstairs.

"Sheng-ge, you're in Class B, right?" the boy asked.

Sheng Wang nodded. The boy pulled a long face. "Fine, at least it's just downstairs, only a ceiling between us."

"You're not?" Sheng Wang asked.

"I've got to go to Class 1," he said. "Who knows if I'll ever fight my way back."

"What are you talking about, of course you will!" Gao Tianyang consoled him.

The boy, for his part, was clear-eyed about it. He said morosely, "Every time someone leaves, this is probably how they get consoled, right? In the end, how many actually come back?"

Gao Tianyang choked, then clapped him on the back. "Then you've got to show some grit!"

He bumped fists with Sheng Wang too. "Sheng-ge, you also—no, wait, don't show too much grit and scare the rest of us."

After he said it, Gao Tianyang reflexively shot a glance at Jiang Tian. He'd expected to be fixed under that stare, the way he had been the time he'd said "just passing through"—but this time Jiang Tian didn't look up.

He keenly sensed some subtle change between the two of them, but with his coelenterate-grade brain, he couldn't begin to describe where the change actually was.

So he chose to shut up and keep quiet as a hen.

Sheng Wang stuffed part of his things into his bag and was about to scoop up another stack of books when Jiang Tian bent down and gathered them up for him, then turned and headed for the stairwell.

Rankings were the sort of thing each class kept behind closed doors; before the classroom switch, nobody knew the situation in the other classes.

Class B was sweeping off the empty desks, waiting for the people from upstairs to descend, and never imagined the first to descend would be Jiang Tian. The student on cleaning duty was so startled the rag slipped in his grip and nearly swiped across someone else's face.

"What's going on?" someone murmured. "What kind of breaking news is this, Jiang Tian's switching classes?"

"Dream on," another scoffed. "Obviously he's just helping someone carry stuff."

"Who's got that kind of clout?"

Just as they were talking, Sheng Wang followed in through the classroom door, bag over his shoulder, and the crowd went dumb all over again.

A few seconds later, someone muttered under their breath, "There. The clout's arrived."

There were a few empty desks. Jiang Tian asked Sheng Wang, "Where do you want to sit?"

"Here!" A dark hand suddenly shot out in front of one of the empty desks. Sheng Wang looked over to see Shi Yu pointing at the seat in front of his own. "Sit here."

"Sure," Sheng Wang nodded.

Jiang Tian said, "Is he taller than you?"

Shi Yu: "……Let's not nitpick over that kind of thing, we're about the same, Tian-ge."

Jiang Tian said no more. He walked over and set Sheng Wang's books down. The other transferring students were trickling in too, claiming the remaining desks. Sheng Wang shoved his bag into the desk compartment and was about to start pulling things out when he heard Jiang Tian say, "I'm heading up."

His movements paused. He looked up. "Sure."

He watched Jiang Tian walk out the back door of the classroom and vanish down the corridor. In that instant, he suddenly remembered seeing the suitcase in the next bedroom over, and a certain break between classes when Jiang Tian had said to him, at the back of the room, "We'll have to move out eventually."

Only this time, it was Sheng Wang who'd gone downstairs.

You chose to walk a little farther away yourself; you're the one who wanted to come down here—so don't go putting on a hypocritical show of not wanting to part.

Sheng Wang told himself this.

There were still ten-some minutes left of the lunch break. The students who'd shifted into Class B had all settled in, and the room slowly went quiet again. The grouping of clusters here was a little different—unfamiliar gaps, unfamiliar faces, and the unfamiliar scent of cleaning solution drifting around.

But it didn't matter. He'd transferred schools so many times, changed so many classrooms; this was just one more of them.

He adapted well, could survive anywhere. It'd take him only a few minutes to get used to this place, just like when he'd transferred across provinces into Class A.

There was still a trace of the stomachache left. Sheng Wang got his things in order and lay down on the desk.

He'd meant to shut his eyes and rest for the tail end of the lunch break, but he accidentally fell asleep. Just as sometimes, no matter how carefully you've planned, there will always be people and things that fall outside the plan.

Class A was a place in the grade both envied and dreaded, so even some of the students who'd tested into the top 45 hesitated, not daring to go in.

The people from Class B and Class 1 had mostly finished shifting, but those few desks in Class A still sat empty. When Jiang Tian got back to the room, he saw several people loitering by the door, craning their necks.

Gao Tianyang once again shouldered the heavy responsibility of social butterfly. He waved at the people outside. "What's the matter, friends, standing at attention? We've cleared the desks for you and you still won't come in—or should I put on a little welcoming parade?"

"No need, no need, no need." The students, faces flushed red, came shuffling in awkwardly with their bags.

"Just pick wherever." Gao Tianyang pointed out a few empty desks, and was about to point to Sheng Wang's when his Tian-ge opened his golden mouth: "Hold on."

Gao Tianyang looked at him, baffled.

Jiang Tian, back in the room, hadn't sat down. Instead he pulled the bag, pencil case, and papers out of the desk compartment. He was tall; one reach and he tossed the few books on the desktop over to the desk in front, then carried his bag and sat down in Sheng Wang's seat.

Gao Tianyang had never seen this maneuver before. He stared, head full of question marks, then asked, "Tian-ge, what are you doing?"

"Switching seats. Can't you tell?" Jiang Tian said.

"No—I mean, I can tell. But—" Gao Tianyang scratched at the short bristles on his crown. "—why are you suddenly switching seats?"

Jiang Tian put his things into the desk compartment one by one and said, without looking up, "I sat here in the first place. Problem?"

Only then did Gao Tianyang remember—before Sheng Wang came, Jiang Tian really had sat here. Now that Sheng Wang had shifted away, he'd carried his things back to this spot.

He felt a sudden pang of something, then quickly came back to himself. "No problem, switching back is good too. Saves me from wanting to whisper in class, leaning back toward the seat behind me, and getting completely ignored by a new classmate. That'd be pretty awkward."

Jiang Tian got his things settled and gave him a look. "I won't pay attention to you either."

"I know that. You not only ignore me, you tell me to shut up and turn my head back around." Gao Tianyang shook his head. "Compared like that, Sheng-ge's still the one who gives me face."

Jiang Tian pressed his lips together and said nothing more. He pulled out a book at random, picked out a gel pen, and didn't lift his head again. Gao Tianyang turned back around with a long sigh, traded a couple of jabs with Song Sirui, and started working through a practice paper too.

Most of the students were grabbing the chance to sleep. The class monitor quietly switched off two of the overhead lights, and the room dimmed. Outside, wind drove the rain slantwise, water roaring everywhere, but inside it was quiet, the same as every lunch break that had come before.

These few competition problems had long prompts and convoluted phrasing. Jiang Tian read for several minutes without a single word going in, and only then realized he was distracted.

He leaned back against the chair, one hand hanging at his side, the other resting on the desk with a pen pinched between his fingers. He spun it four or five times and still couldn't take in a single problem, until at last he gave up and lifted his eyes.

The back leaning against the desk in front had been replaced by Gao Tianyang—no longer the one who, when it got hot, liked to peel his uniform down off his shoulders and laze about airing out the collar of his T-shirt. Nor was there anyone braced on the desk's crossbar now, idly rocking the chair, knocking lightly against the edge of his desk now and then, then turning back with a grin to make nice and apologize.

He lowered his gaze and drifted off for a while, struck suddenly by the sense that he'd gone all the way around a great loop and arrived back at the starting point—as if the transfer student wedged in the middle of it had never come at all.

If he didn't turn around, didn't look at the few new classmates who'd shifted in, he could almost have an illusion. As if he'd merely lain down on his desk over the lunch break and slept, and dreamed a short, slight dream.

When he'd closed his eyes it was high summer. When he opened them it was already deep autumn.

The phone screen in his bag suddenly lit up. Jiang Tian reflexively pulled it out and opened WeChat—but there was no new message on the screen. It took him a moment to realize it was just some app's midday news push.

He swiped the notification away and looked, silent, at the very top of his WeChat list, where that flat little Wangzai sticker sat quietly inside the avatar frame.

The truth was, Jiang Tian had always had the habit of setting custom names for his contacts—plain, dull style, just the person's full name or title. The one at the top was his first exception.

He'd briefly changed it to "Sheng Wang," and then, late one night a few days later, some unknown impulse had made him change it back. At the time he couldn't have said what was behind it. Now he could put a little more of it into words—he just wanted to be able to see the other person's changes. Whether he'd switched his avatar or not. Whether he was happy or not.

He suddenly remembered a noon several years ago, an overcast, rainy day just like this one, when that cat called "Captain" had passed away peacefully in its basket.

There had been plenty of signs before that—it stopped eating, stopped wanting to move. He'd run to many shops, searched many websites, tried many methods, hoping to keep it around a few more years.

Old Man Ding had said, "It's an old cat. Its time's about up. There's no keeping it."

And in the end, sure enough, there was no keeping it.

……

It always seemed to go like this.

As a child he'd tied Jiang Ou's armband around his finger, only to open his eyes and never see the person. Later he'd made his own name and photo into a little note and tied it around his grandmother's wrist, and still the old woman couldn't remember him. Later still he'd taken many photos and videos of Captain, and that cat, who had kept him company for a long time, was buried in the ground all the same.

He had never been good at holding on, and had never held on to anything.

These past few days Sheng Wang had started calling him "ge" often, but it didn't make him happy. Instead it kept dredging up these old, bygone things. He knew that the person who had once hooked an arm around his neck and said "let's room together" was walking off into the distance—and he didn't know how to keep him.

So many years had passed, and he still hadn't learned how to hold on. He still only knew a few stiff, stubborn, foolish methods.

They had never once worked. But he still wanted to try.
