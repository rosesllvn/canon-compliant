Canon Compliant

Chapter 63

Declaration

A Certain Someone (某某) by Musuli — fan translation

"Jiang Tian knows?!" Sheng Wang froze.

Zhao Xi nodded. "Mm."

The book spun out of Sheng Wang's hand. He sat there blankly for a long moment before bending to scoop it up, and asked again, unable to believe it, "Jiang Tian knows?"

Zhao Xi: "……"

He couldn't help laughing. "You didn't drop your book when I came to talk to you—what are you dropping it for now?"

Sheng Wang didn't answer. Instead he genuinely blanked for a very, very long time.

Scene after scene flashed rapidly through his mind—the two of them, the four of them, the whole crowd of them. They all froze, in the end, on the same line: more than one person had said that he and Jiang Tian were a lot like Zhao Xi and Lin Beiting. The speakers meant nothing by it; the listener took it to heart. Sheng Wang had heard it who knew how many times—and nearly every time, Jiang Tian had been right there beside him.

So how could he possibly know?

It wasn't possible.

Sheng Wang thought, dazed.

It wasn't possible…

Otherwise, how could he have heard it so many times and never once refuted it?

"How is it not possible?" Zhao Xi suddenly spoke up. Sheng Wang looked at him and only then realized he'd said not possible out loud.

"Isn't it perfectly normal that Jiang Tian knows? How many years have he and I known each other?" Zhao Xi said with feeling. "He was still little when I was in high school. I won't say I felt nothing—now that it comes up, I've actually seen him when he was that small? Pretty amazing."

He spoke of everything with a smile, whether it was him and Lin Beiting or him and Jiang Tian, as if it were all just idle chat. But the more he said, the more of a mess Sheng Wang's heart became.

Right—Jiang Tian had lived at Wutongwai since he was small, and Zhao Xi was from here too. They'd known each other so many years and were still so close; of course he'd know, nothing could be more natural.

But—if he knew about Zhao Xi and Lin Beiting's relationship, then every time he heard those remarks about the two of them being alike, what had he been thinking?

And why was he always so silent?

Sheng Wang thought: Was he afraid that refuting it would leave me with nowhere to put my face? Or…

What came after "or" was too absurd. He knew he shouldn't let himself think it, but he couldn't help thinking it anyway. And so his heart, which had sunk all the way to the bottom, drifted gently up again on that faint, barely-there notion.

He suddenly felt rather hypocritical—he'd warned himself again and again, that's my ge, and yet in the end, the moment he thought of a one-in-a-hundred-million absurd possibility, he couldn't help growing happy again. Even though the possibility was so small it could be discounted entirely, and would never be verified.

He stared at some point in empty space and spaced out for a long time, then finally asked Zhao Xi, "Xi-ge, did he know even when he was very small?"

"You mean Jiang Tian?"

"Mm."

Zhao Xi recalled for a moment. "When Lin and I first got together he didn't know. He was too little then, around five or six. I used to run errands for my dad delivering things to the mute uncle, and Jiang Tian was always over at Old Master Ding's place across the way."

"His surname isn't Ding, I think," Sheng Wang said.

"Right, though probably hardly anyone actually knows the old master's real surname—he rarely brings it up." Zhao Xi quirked up one corner of his mouth in a wicked little grin. "The nickname 'Old Man Ding' was even mine to start with. Some of the kids in the alley plagiarized it off me later, and after that this generation all called him that."

"Everyone calls him that? Then the first time I called him 'Grandpa Ding,' why'd his eyes go that wide?"

"He was just messing with you. The old master's got a temper, but he's a good sort."

Zhao Xi was sitting at Jiang Tian's desk, and he absently fished a ruler out of the pencil case and toyed with it in his hand. "Jiang Tian used to read books in the old man's courtyard a lot back then. Wasn't very old and had a terribly stubborn temper—I thought right away, this kid's going to be proud when he grows up, and definitely the closed-off type too."

"I was pretty wild then, didn't have much patience. Sometimes I'd tease him a couple of times and walk off, sometimes I'd chat with him a while. At first he ignored me, but later he hit a book he couldn't understand, so I went over and showed off with a whole spiel. He'd probably never met a little hoodlum who liked reading—found it novel, I guess—so he grudgingly paid me a bit of attention. After that we slowly got familiar, and I brought Lin around to meet him too. Lin was a famous campus tyrant back in middle school, never had a good face on him all day; the sight of him sitting across from Jiang Tian was genuinely hilarious."

Sheng Wang thought of the Jiang Tian in Old Man Ding's telling. Those two years Zhao Xi spoke of were exactly when Jiang Tian had been shut out by his grandmother. With that prickly temperament of his, for him to become openly familiar with Zhao Xi and Lin Beiting meant he'd only have valued it more inside. They'd probably been among the very few friends he had in that period.

"Back then Jiang Tian didn't know. It was later—university, I think? I can't remember exactly which year anymore. Once during a break I came back to tidy up my things, wanting to find some suitable books for Jiang Tian to read, and ended up digging out a fair bit of old junk—among it two Polaroid photos, tucked right into an old book." Zhao Xi recalled for a moment, then laughed ruefully. "By then Lin and I weren't together anymore, and seeing the photos out of nowhere threw me a little too. I didn't put them away right away, and Jiang Tian saw them."

Seeing the puzzlement on Sheng Wang's face, he added, "The photos were about on the level of what you walked in on the other day."

Sheng Wang gave an awkward "Oh," indicating he understood.

Zhao Xi raised a brow—this delinquent truly lived up to his campus-tyrant origins; as the party involved, he wasn't the least bit embarrassed. "Jiang Tian wasn't very old then either, under ten I think. I figured he wouldn't understand at all. Didn't expect the kid to react so strongly."

"React strongly?" Sheng Wang didn't get it at first.

Zhao Xi thought it over. "Extremely, extremely repelled."

Sheng Wang froze.

That one-in-a-hundred-million absurd possibility vanished abruptly in those few words of Zhao Xi's, like a balloon pricked through—after it burst, only a few scattered remnants drifted slowly down, falling silently to the floor.

After who knew how long, he asked softly, "Very… repelled?"

"Mm. Repelled enough that he left without even taking the book," Zhao Xi said. "He was young then, different from now. However hard he held it in, you could still read it on his face. I could tell he was forcing himself to bear it out of politeness, but I could also tell he felt extremely…"

He knit his brows, weighing the word. For a moment Sheng Wang suspected he'd say "disgusted," but what he finally said was "uncomfortable."

Zhao Xi said the Jiang Tian of that time had looked extremely uncomfortable.

"So like I said, your reaction today surprised me." Zhao Xi's light-brown eyes looked at Sheng Wang, the ruler turning back and forth in his hand. "It's a world of difference from Jiang Tian. Though his sort is rare too—most people who learn about this, their reaction at the time falls somewhere between the two of yours."

Sheng Wang lowered his gaze and gave a smile that was half self-mockery, half playing along. "Is that so. Then we really are brothers—each took one of the two extremes."

"Pretty extreme, yeah. That kid nearly made me doubt my whole existence at the time," Zhao Xi said, half joking. "After he left I spent a whole day in self-reflection, thinking, is it really that bad? Is it really that hard to accept?"

"And then what?" Sheng Wang asked.

"Then? Then I told myself, the little brat's just a pain, why should I have to coax him—let him be. And not two days later, I went meekly off to talk to him." Zhao Xi tipped up his chin. "Just like I'm talking to you now, except not this relaxed. He was so closed-off, wouldn't say a single thought he had, and I had no idea whether my talking was having any effect."

"At the time I even suspected he had some kind of psychological trauma. Later I figured he probably really had run into a few things."

Sheng Wang's eyes snapped up, but Zhao Xi had no intention of going deeper. "It's a guess, nothing I have any basis for, so I won't say it to you. Anyway, I did my best back then, talked with him many times. Not long after that he moved away from here, and I went abroad. We kept in touch, but not much. Then after a year and a bit, getting on two years, I came back to spend a summer—he made a few trips to Wutongwai. The first few times he said it was to see Old Man Ding, and then he finally came looking for me on his own, apologized to me all stiff and awkward. That's when I knew he'd worked it through."

He'd worked it through.

Those four words were said lightly enough, but Zhao Xi knew that for someone with a temperament like Jiang Tian's, turning some ingrained conviction around over the better part of two years had surely involved no small amount of back-and-forth and struggle.

It was also from that day that he'd realized—to Jiang Tian, he and Lin Beiting really were very important friends.

"I always say he's a bit too old for his years. That's not quite it, though. When he gets proud he's a match for the brat I used to be—a lot of the time he's pretty asking-for-a-beating, just trades on that face of his." Zhao Xi gave a couple of "tsk"s, then said in a low voice, "But he's extremely rational. Never mind people his own age—people much older than him can't necessarily reason their way to this. He won't take one person's problem and spread it across a whole group. That's a rare thing."

As he talked, Zhao Xi looked up and found that Sheng Wang had long since drifted off. He didn't know what part Sheng Wang had heard or what he'd thought of; maybe it was the cold cast of the classroom lights, which lit his face a pale, drained white.

The reaction was genuinely a bit abnormal, and linking it with certain earlier details, Zhao Xi gradually began to frown. He looked at the boy's slightly lowered brow and eyes and suddenly called out, low, "Sheng Wang?"

"Hm?" Sheng Wang came back to himself and looked up.

"You were spacing out, and your color's not great—not feeling well?" Zhao Xi said.

"No." Sheng Wang shook his head. "Just thought of something a second ago. Unrelated."

"Good then," Zhao Xi said.

As they talked, Sheng Wang suddenly noticed a new-message alert on his phone screen, received two minutes ago. He unlocked it and tapped into WeChat. The message was from Jiang Tian—

Sacred Tree of Changbai Mountain: I'm done over here

Sacred Tree of Changbai Mountain: Wait for you downstairs?

Sheng Wang looked at the contact name, his expression complicated, and typed back: coming.

Zhao Xi asked, "Jiang Tian wrapped up over there?"

Sheng Wang nodded. "Mm."

"Then let's go, downstairs." He stood up from the desk, not forgetting to put the ruler he'd been fiddling with for ages back into Jiang Tian's pencil case.

Sheng Wang followed behind him, finding the contact name more and more of an eyesore the longer he looked. So he changed it to "Best Actor of the Forest"—no telling whether he was poking fun at Jiang Tian or at himself.

The cold lights in the classroom suddenly dimmed. Sheng Wang looked up to see Zhao Xi switching them off. He'd just finished editing the name and tapped confirm when Zhao Xi, up ahead, suddenly turned his head and asked, "Sheng Wang, actually I've been wanting to ask—you wouldn't happen to also…"

He said it hesitantly, obliquely, but Sheng Wang understood almost at once. His heart gave a jolt, and reflexively he flashed Zhao Xi a smile. "What are you thinking, Xi-ge? I like girls."

Zhao Xi looked down at him, his gaze for once without its roguishness, carrying instead a touch of gentleness. He nodded. "Ah, good then."

Sheng Wang blanked.

"It's not an easy road, this one," Zhao Xi added, as if in reflection, and as if speaking to him.

"I know," Sheng Wang said, reaching out to pull the classroom door handle.

The door opened—and there was Jiang Tian, leaning against the doorframe, head bent, scrolling his phone. No telling how long he'd been standing there, or how much he'd heard.

Sheng Wang thought back over what he'd just said, and only one word was left in his heart—damn.
